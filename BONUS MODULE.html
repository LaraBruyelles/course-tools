Life Onboard — The Real Picture

Before you sign, you need to know what your daily life will actually look like. Here's the honest version — the good, the genuinely hard parts, and the things nobody usually talks about.

A Typical Day as a Solo Musician

Port Day: Wake up, explore the destination, return to ship, rest, get ready. Perform your sets in the evening. That's pretty much it.

Sea Day: More time onboard. Perform your sets. Occasional crew duties — emergency drills (approx. once a week, ~1 hour, life jacket, stand by your station) and sometimes passenger muster at the start of a cruise on some ships. On American ships, contracted solo musicians often have fewer duties than regular crew.

Typical schedule: 2–4 sets of ~45 minutes, 6 days per week, 1 day off. (RC lists 4–5 sets/day — in practice 3–4 is most common for contracted soloists.)

River cruises: If you're the only musician on a very small river cruise ship, you may have additional non-musical duties. Confirm your role clearly before signing.

💡 The Most Important Mindset Shift

You are not just performing. You are hosting the room — taking requests, talking to guests, reading the energy, creating a party atmosphere. Especially on American ships, this is the job. If this excites you, you'll thrive.

Your Status Onboard (Especially on American Ships)

✓ Contracted Solo Musicians Often Get Special Privileges

✓ Access to passenger restaurants (most crew cannot eat there)

✓ Better cabin — typically a double bed with porthole or outside window

✓ Sometimes exempt from wearing a crew ID tag   ✓ Sometimes included on the guest list

✓ Tip jar permitted for piano vocalists and guitar vocalists on American ships

✓ On Royal Caribbean, I was able to display my Instagram handle on the piano — passengers followed me and I built a real following. Not all cruise lines allow this.

✓ Some musicians sell CDs through the ship's onboard shop — the cruise line takes a commission, but it's possible.

These privileges vary between cruise lines and ship types. Always confirm what applies to your specific contract.

Types of Ships — It's Not Just One World

Different Ships, Different Experiences

Large mainstream ships (Royal Caribbean, Carnival, Norwegian): High energy, large audiences, lively atmosphere. Great for building performance confidence.

Luxury / ultra-luxury lines (Silversea, Seabourn, Azamara): Smaller ships, refined audiences, often better pay. Less high-energy atmosphere, more polished performance expected.

Expedition ships: Smaller vessels, remote and incredible itineraries (Arctic, Galapagos, Amazon). Unique experiences beyond typical destinations.

River cruises: Smaller ships on rivers. Intimate setting, potentially extra duties on very small ships with just one musician onboard.

Read both sides carefully. The musicians who struggle most are the ones who weren't prepared for the emotional and psychological reality. The ones who thrive knew what they were getting into — and went anyway.

Is This For You?

✓ Are energized by performing for an audience every night   ✓ Want financial stability and peace of mind

✓ Love exploring new countries   ✓ Can handle 4–6 months away from home

✓ Are ready to host a room and take requests   ✓ Want to accelerate your professional growth

⚠ Think carefully if you…

✗ Find it very hard to have your personal and professional life in the same space constantly

Life on Board: The Honest Prep

Practical preparation for what life on a ship actually looks like — what to pack, how to stay connected, how to take care of yourself, and how to be someone people genuinely want to work with.

What to Pack — Performance Wardrobe

Your Stage Wardrobe Matters

On most cruise lines, you perform almost every night for months. You need multiple performance outfits — at least 5–7 complete looks so you're not repeating the same outfit every few days in front of the same crew and returning passengers.

Calibrate to the line: luxury lines require elegant, understated outfits; American mainstream lines want polished but energetic; Disney requires presentable and family-appropriate at all times.

When in doubt: slightly overdressed beats underdressed. You are always representing the cruise line as well as yourself.

Pack List Checklist

Ships use various plug types. A universal adapter is essential.

Bring only what you actually take. Medical care is available onboard — you don't need to predict or research everything. If you get sick, you'll be taken care of.

Your voice is your instrument — take care of it proactively.

Cabin practice is fine for guitar. Piano practice is restricted — you can only use the piano when no guests are around, which is nearly impossible on busy ships. Many piano entertainers learn requests mentally and play by ear. A small rolling keyboard lets you learn new songs while on board without disturbing anyone.

Sandals aren't allowed in below-deck crew corridors, but you'll use these corridors multiple times daily to reach crew dining and facilities. Crocs or easy slip-ons are essential.

You need things that give you mental space away from performing.

Get a Wi-Fi package once you're on board. Crew Wi-Fi is cheaper than passenger Wi-Fi, and prices vary by ship. They'll explain the options on your first day.

WhatsApp messaging is often included free for crew — text messages go through at no cost. But as soon as you try to send voice messages, photos, videos, or open social media, you've left the free tier. The basic crew plan covers text only. Budget for a paid package if you want anything beyond that.

Homesickness & Staying Connected

Plan for It Before You Go

Homesickness is real, and it hits most people — especially on their first contract and especially during long sea days. The emotional intensity is not a sign that you're doing it wrong. It's completely normal.

Practical tips:

✓ Set up scheduled video calls with people at home before you leave — something to look forward to each week

✓ Get a Wi-Fi package once you're onboard — crew Wi-Fi is cheaper than passenger rates and they'll explain options on day one

✓ Tell the people who matter to you what the contract means — and how they can help you succeed in it

✓ Have at least one person you can message at any hour when things feel hard

✓ Accept that conversations will sometimes be short and choppy — Wi-Fi on a ship is not the same as home

Relationship Impact — Be Honest With Yourself

⚠️ Have the Honest Conversation Before You Leave

Long contracts are genuinely hard on relationships. Not impossible — but hard. Before you sail, have a real conversation with your partner, family, or anyone who will be significantly affected.

Questions worth discussing:

→ How will we stay connected realistically, given time zones and Wi-Fi costs?

→ What happens if one of us really struggles during the contract?

→ What does "coming home" look like — is there a plan for the re-integration period?

Many relationships survive cruise ship contracts — and many are strengthened by them. Planning and communication before you go makes all the difference.

Mental Health & Isolation

It's Normal to Struggle — and Normal to Ask for Help

Living where you work is psychologically unusual. You can never fully switch off. Your boss is at the crew bar. A passenger you had a difficult night with is at the buffet next morning. After months, the constant proximity to work and the same faces can become genuinely exhausting.

Solo musicians are particularly vulnerable to this — you perform alone, you often finish late when the rest of the crew has already socialised, and isolation is easy to slip into.

Proactive self-care on a ship:

✓ Find one or two people you genuinely connect with — not the whole crew, just your people

✓ Protect your mornings or your one day off as personal time — genuinely yours

✓ Use port days to actually decompress, not just to see sights

✓ If you're struggling, the ship's medical team can help — you are entitled to mental health support under your employment

✓ Know that finishing a contract and feeling ready to go home is not failure — it's wisdom

Being a Good Ship Citizen

✓ Your Conduct Matters As Much As Your Music

How you behave onboard — with crew, staff, passengers, and management — directly affects your career. Musicians who are reliable, easy to work with, and genuinely respectful get rehired, get better contracts, and build a reputation that opens doors.

✓ Be on time — every time. For performances, for drills, for everything.

✓ Be friendly and professional with all crew, not just your peers.

✓ Handle difficult requests or audience moments with grace.

✓ If something goes wrong, go through the right channels calmly. Let your agency advocate if needed.

✓ Your reputation follows you — agents, cruise directors, and bookers talk. Be someone people want back.

Ship Rules, Hierarchy & Culture

Timing & Arrival

Arrive on your assigned day and time. Lateness can result in a formal warning or immediate termination. This is treated as a serious breach.

Be on time for every single set and performance. No exceptions, no excuses. This is one of the most important professional habits you can build.

Guests Always Come First — Even When It's Hard

If you are queuing, entering a lift, or moving through shared spaces and guests are waiting — you wait. You cannot go in front of guests under any circumstances. Even running late for a set, you plan ahead so you never cut in front of a guest.

This also means: if a guest is rude, dismissive, or difficult — you cannot respond the way you might on land. You smile, stay professional, and absorb it. This is one of the genuinely harder parts of the job. Some passengers treat crew like staff in the worst sense of the word. If you react badly, even to someone being unfair to you, it can cost you your contract.

Crew Rules & The Hierarchy

No more than 4 crew members together at any time — at the buffet, socialising, anywhere. Crew cannot form large groups in public areas.

Always wear your crew ID/name tag when required. Even when you'd rather not. The rule is the rule.

Understand the chain of command. Officers, staff, crew — there's structure for a reason. Knowing who to approach for what, and doing it through the right channels, protects you and makes you someone who's easy to work with.

Security duty: You'll be assigned an emergency role when you board. Learn the ship's emergency codes (announced on the speakers) and know your station. This is mandatory for all crew, not optional.

Hierarchy matters more on a ship than most land-based jobs. It doesn't have to feel oppressive — but understanding and respecting it is part of being a professional at sea.

The honest truth about fairness: things will sometimes be unfair. I once received a warning that made no sense and had no basis. There was nothing I could do about it. When you are in a strong hierarchy environment at sea, your options are limited — and pushing back aggressively can result in termination. You learn to pick your battles, document things, escalate through your agency when something is genuinely wrong, and absorb the rest. It's not ideal. It's the reality of this environment.

Alcohol, Drugs & Random Testing

🚨 This Can End Your Contract Immediately

Random drug and alcohol tests happen on cruise ships with no warning. You can be called to the medical centre any morning. If you test positive, you will be disembarked immediately. I personally knew musicians who lost their contracts this way.

Alcohol limits: The general rule is no more than one drink (often one beer) per day for crew members. The exact rule varies by cruise line but the principle is the same — you are essentially sober on the job, always.

Contracted solo musicians often have slightly more relaxed social privileges than regular crew — you may be allowed in passenger areas and have more freedom off-duty. But the testing rules apply to you equally. "More privileges" does not mean different drug and alcohol rules.

Weed / cannabis: Even in ports or countries where it is legal, you are subject to the ship's rules and the flag state's laws. A positive test is a positive test, regardless of where you consumed it.

This isn't meant to lecture anyone. It's meant to make sure you go in knowing the stakes. One random test on a Tuesday morning can end a four-month contract.

Your First Week On Board

What to Expect When You First Board

The first few days are often overwhelming. After embarkation day chaos, you'll start navigating the actual ship life. Here's what to be ready for:

Who to report to: Your first point of contact onboard is usually the Cruise Director (CD) or the Entertainment Manager. Introduce yourself early, be warm and professional, and ask directly: "Is there anything you need from me this week beyond my scheduled sets?" This one question puts you immediately in a different category from most musicians.

Your performance schedule is broader than you expect: It's not just your lounge sets. You may be asked to provide background music for embarkation, accompany guest entertainers, play for events (cocktail parties, themed nights), or fill in for other acts. Stay flexible. Say yes when you can.

Sea days change everything: On port days, most guests are off the ship and you perform to smaller crowds. On sea days, the ship is full and the programming demand increases significantly. Musicians who can offer activities on sea days — trivia with music, name-that-tune, singalongs, musical bingo — are valued by cruise directors far beyond their evening sets. If you have any of these skills, mention them early.

When problems arise: Equipment failures, scheduling conflicts, misunderstandings — go to your Entertainment Manager first, then contact your agency if unresolved. Never go over people's heads without exhausting the chain of command. And if something genuinely serious happens (medical emergency, serious misconduct), your agency and the ship's medical/HR team are your resources.

On-Board Opportunities

Welcome Shows & Special Moments

Beyond your regular sets, opportunities arise that can be genuinely significant for your career:

Welcome shows: I did a welcome show where I had a 6-minute medley — the whole stage to myself with 500 people in the theater and my name on the screen behind me. It was huge for me personally and professionally. These moments position you as a serious entertainer beyond the lounge. They also get mentioned in guest reviews, which affects your assessment and renewal.

Shared sets with other crew musicians: You can sometimes partner with another musician to co-host a show or share a performance slot. It's optional — and it adds to your schedule if you choose it — but it's a chance to build musical relationships, network within the crew, and create memorable moments that guests write about.

These opportunities are not guaranteed on every ship or every contract — but saying yes when they arise is almost always worth it.

The People Side: Crew Friendships & Goodbyes

Something Nobody Really Warns You About

My first contract, I threw myself into friendships with everyone. And then someone left — their contract ended. And then another person. And then another. You start to realise that people leave the ship every single week, and eventually you become somewhat numb to it. Sometimes you don't even say a proper goodbye anymore because it happens so often.

This isn't coldness — it's adaptation. But it's worth knowing going in, because the intensity of connection on a ship is real, and the transience of it is equally real. People you feel genuinely close to can leave your daily world in a day. Some you'll stay in touch with forever. Most you won't.

Build the connections. Enjoy them fully. And hold them lightly.

Embarkation Day — The Most Chaotic Day

⚠️ Prepare for Controlled Chaos

No matter the ship, embarkation day is almost always overwhelming. You arrive having probably not slept well (pre-embarkation stress is real), and then you're immediately asked to:

✓ Collect your transported suitcases   ✓ Sign multiple documents   ✓ Complete mandatory safety training   ✓ Meet your team and get briefed   ✓ Possibly perform on your first evening

Everything happens at once. People give you information you can't possibly retain. You're physically and emotionally exhausted before the contract has even started.

⚠️ Do not arrive without your documents: If you show up to the ship missing a required document — your passport, medical certificate, STCW, visa, or cruise line forms — you can be refused boarding on the spot. Have physical printed copies of everything, plus offline digital backups on your phone. Don't rely on emails you can't open without Wi-Fi.

The reassurance: after 2–3 days it settles. The chaos is temporary. Every experienced ship musician went through the same thing on their first day. You will find your rhythm.

Coming Home: The Post-Contract Reality

Re-Entry Is Its Own Challenge

Nobody really prepares you for how hard it can be to return to land life after a contract. Some things that catch people off guard:

The food adjustment: Ship food is very specific. You get used to it — some people love it, some don't — but after months, you're conditioned to it. Normal cooking and groceries feel strange at first.

The rhythm of land life: Not having to cook, clean, pay for anything, or handle daily admin for months creates a particular kind of ease. Returning to bills, chores, and ordinary logistics can feel genuinely jarring — not because those things are hard, but because the contrast is so stark.

The accumulation of tiredness: The fatigue often only fully hits once you're home. You've been performing, socialising, adapting, and being "on" for months. The crash, when it comes, can be significant.

The long-term pattern: In the long run, some musicians find it genuinely difficult to return to land because it's hard to replicate the financial ease, the sense of being taken care of, and the intensity of ship life. Even now, after years away from cruise ships, I sometimes miss the simplicity and the luxury of that position — waking up in a new country, eating in beautiful restaurants, never cleaning, always performing. It's a different world, and coming back from it takes real adjustment.

Give yourself time after a contract. Don't rush straight back into building everything at once. Your nervous system needs to decompress.

Protecting Yourself

Your rights and personal safety — beyond the financial contract checks. This is about being empowered, not scared.

💡 You Have Rights as a Crew Member

Working on a ship feels unusual legally — you're at sea, on a vessel flagged in another country, managed by an agency. But you are not without rights. International maritime labor law exists specifically to protect people in your position.

The Maritime Labour Convention (MLC 2006)

The MLC 2006 is the international framework that sets minimum standards for seafarers. Most major cruise lines are bound by it. Key protections:

Hours of rest: Minimum rest periods that employers must respect.

Medical care: You are entitled to medical care onboard at no cost while you are crew.

Repatriation rights: If you are genuinely unable to continue due to illness or emergency, you have the right to be sent home at the cruise line's expense. I have experienced this myself — the system can work for you when you genuinely need it.

Clear contract terms: Your employment terms must be in writing and in a language you understand.

Verify specifics with your agency for your specific contract. Standards and enforcement vary between lines.

When Things Go Wrong Onboard

First: Contact your Entertainment Manager or direct line manager onboard.

Second: Contact your agency. A good agency will advocate for you directly. This is part of what you're paying their commission for.

Third: For welfare or safety issues, the ship's medical team and HR department are your internal resources.

For genuine emergencies: Repatriation is your legal right under MLC if you are genuinely unwell or unsafe. You do not have to stay onboard in a situation that is genuinely harming you.

I have personally been repatriated from a contract. It felt like a very big decision at the time — but it was the right one, and the system worked.

🚨 Personal Red Flags Beyond the Financial Ones

❌ Vague or missing terms about your cabin type or status — you should know exactly where you'll live before you sign.

❌ No repatriation clause — any legitimate seafarer contract should include this.

❌ Pressure to sign immediately on a contract (not just a verbal offer).

❌ "Exclusivity forever" clauses that prevent you from working with any other agent at all.

❌ An agent who won't put things in writing after confirming them verbally.

❌ An agent you can't reach or who doesn't respond consistently — if they're hard to contact before you sign, imagine when you're at sea.

⚠️ Trust Your Gut

No checklist covers everything. If an offer feels off — the energy of the agent, something vague in the language, an evasive response — slow down and verify before committing. You are not obligated to sign anything until you are genuinely confident.

Where This Can Take You Next

Your first cruise ship contract is not the endpoint — it's a launching pad.

Hotels, Resorts & Land-Based Residencies

Much of what this course teaches — your promo video, repertoire, professional approach — applies directly to hotel and resort work internationally. Many agencies that recruit for cruise ships also place musicians in hotels, resorts, and destination venues worldwide. The pay is usually lower than cruise ships, but it comes with a more normal life alongside the work.

Cruise ship experience carries enormous weight. Having played 300+ songs for international audiences positions you above almost any other applicant for piano bar residencies, corporate events, private functions, and luxury venue bookings.

International & Destination Gigs

Ship experience is recognized internationally in ways that local gigging experience isn't. Having worked on ships for a major cruise line signals to bookers and venues worldwide that you are professional, reliable, and capable of performing to a high standard night after night. Local performing in destination cities is also a growing and underserved niche — musicians with ship experience who stay in or return to specific regions can build real careers in places others haven't thought to look.

✓ Guest Entertainer & Headliner Path

As you develop as a cruise ship musician, you can begin building toward being a guest entertainer or headliner in the main theater. These are typically short contracts (2–3 weeks), significantly better pay than lounge work, and you perform your own show for hundreds or thousands of passengers in a full theater.

The path: Develop a headliner-quality show on the side → film it professionally → build a relationship with cruise directors onboard → the same agencies you're already working with can pitch you for guest entertainer slots once your show is ready.

It's competitive and takes time — but it's real and achievable for musicians who keep developing.

Cruise Ship Experience as a Career Asset

The network and credibility you build from ship work extends further than most musicians realize:

Agents and bookers: Many people who now run agencies started as ship musicians. Having worked in the same world changes how those conversations go.

Teaching and coaching: Real, extensive professional performance experience in a demanding international environment is extremely valuable in music education.

Industry roles: Understanding the cruise world from the inside opens doors on the industry side — agenting, entertainment coordination, artist management.

Whatever direction you take after this, the experience and identity shift that comes from working as a professional musician internationally — that travels with you forever.

Managing Your Career Long-Term

How Good Agents Think About Your Career

The best agents don't just fill a spot and move on. They ask: what do you actually want? Maximum work all year? One contract a year to supplement land income? Try different lines and build toward luxury? Once you're on their books and have proven yourself, this is the conversation to have.

Plan your next contract before the current one ends. Musicians who don't have this managed end up with long unpaid gaps between contracts — sometimes months. A good agent is already thinking about your next placement while you're mid-contract. If yours isn't, ask them to be.

The progression path: Most musicians start on mass-market lines (Royal Caribbean, Carnival, Norwegian) to build their track record. With 1–2 strong contracts behind you, mid-tier lines open up. With a polished, refined repertoire and a professional reputation, luxury and ultra-luxury lines become accessible. This takes time — usually several years — but it's a real and achievable trajectory.

Returning musicians get priority. Cruise lines and agents both prefer to re-hire musicians they already know. Being professional, reliable, and genuinely good at the job means you get invited back — often before a slot is advertised publicly. Your reputation is your most valuable career asset in this industry.

Diversify when you're ready: As your career develops, you can mix contract work with guest entertainer slots (higher per-show fees), local act appearances at ports along regular itineraries, hotel and resort residencies, and land-based work between contracts. These layers build a more resilient and varied income than any single contract type.

A Question Worth Answering Now

What do you actually want from this career — in one year, and in three years?

One contract to fund a project and return to land? A steady rotation of 2–3 contracts per year? Build toward luxury lines? Develop a headliner show? Something else entirely?

Write it down. Tell your agent. It changes how they work for you — and it changes how you make decisions when offers arrive.
