The #1 Reason Musicians Get Ignored

(It's Not Talent)

The most common reason a musician gets ignored is an unprofessional approach — not a lack of talent. Agents see hundreds of applications. The ones that get a response are deliberate, polished, and easy to act on.

If you can't put together a basic professional email, what chance is there that you'll read your schedule, show up on time, dress right, and be reliable on a ship? The email is a proxy for on-board reliability.
— The agent's logic

⚠ Warning: Some agents auto-forward your submission immediately

Some agents forward your promo to every cruise line the moment they receive it — to “claim ownership.” A sloppy, rushed submission can get you burned across the entire market at once. Be deliberate about who you send to.

✗ Avoid
  • • CC’ing 50 agents in one email
  • • “Dear Agent, available from tomorrow”
  • • Generic spray-and-pray emails
  • • Sending before materials are ready
  • • Vague Facebook messages
✓ Be Surgical
  • • One email per agent, written for them
  • • Use their name if you have it
  • • Research the agency before applying
  • • Send only when your reel is polished
  • • State your position clearly
✓ What a Good First Email Looks Like

Simple, honest, personal. Gets straight to the point.

Hi [Agent Name], My name is [Your Name]. I'm a [piano bar entertainer / guitar vocalist] looking for cruise ship contracts. Reel: [public YouTube link] Song list: attached Available from: [Month Year] [Optional] I don't yet have cruise ship experience, but I'd welcome your guidance. [Your Name] · [Phone] · [Email]
Honesty about experience level is completely fine — agents respect it. What they don't respect is vague or disorganised.

How to Choose a Trustworthy Agent

Before you respond to any agent — understand commission, red flags, and the rules that protect you.

✓ Trust Signals — a good agent:
  • ✓ 100% transparent about your salary AND commission
  • ✓ Responsive and reachable
  • ✓ Cares about your career goals
  • ✓ Puts everything in writing
  • ✓ Welcomes your questions
🚩 Red Flags — walk away if:
  • ✗ “We never take commission” — this is a lie
  • ✗ Vague terms or pressure to sign fast
  • ✗ Slow or no communication
  • ✗ “Exclusivity forever” with no clear terms
  • ✗ Can't find any musicians they've placed

How agent commission actually works

Agents are paid by the cruise line, not directly by you — but you feel it in your salary. The line pays the agent (e.g. €1,200/week) and the agent pays you less (e.g. €800/week), keeping the difference. A transparent agent will tell you both numbers upfront. Industry standard for solo musicians is around 15%. Hidden commission is unacceptable.

“We don't charge commission” is a lie — it just means the split is hidden. Always ask for both numbers.

⚠ The First-Submitter Rule — critical to understand

Most cruise lines honour the first agent to submit a musician and ignore later submissions. If two agents submit you to the same line, there will be a dispute — and it damages your reputation with both.

The rule: Always tell each agent exactly which other agencies or cruise lines you have already been submitted to. Never let two agents unknowingly submit you to the same line.

Before You Apply to Cruise Lines: Read This First

🚀 I want to get hired fast

Prioritise agencies. They have existing relationships and can place you faster. Cost: 5–15% commission from your salary.

💰 I want full salary + direct relationship

Apply directly to cruise lines first. Slower, but you keep 100% of your earnings.

The loyalty trap

If a cruise line first hires you through an agency, they will associate you with that agency. Applying directly later means they'll refer you back to your agent. Your first contract sets the relationship — try direct first when possible.

✓ The practical approach

Apply to agencies and cruise lines at the same time. Try direct first for each cruise line. If an agency places you first — accept it. For your first contract, getting in quickly through an agent is often more valuable than waiting months.

How to Follow Up

Most musicians never follow up. The ones who do stand out. Use these templates and set reminders.

Follow-Up Timeline

14d

14 Days After Submission

Send your first follow-up. Keep it brief and professional. Reference your original email and reiterate your availability.

30d

30 Days After Submission

Send one final follow-up. After this, move on — reapply in 3–4 months with updated materials.

3mo

3–4 Months Later

Reapply with fresh materials. Mention what has changed or improved since your last application.
