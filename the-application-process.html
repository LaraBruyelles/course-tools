The #1 Reason Musicians Get Ignored

(It's Not Talent)

The most common reason a musician gets ignored is an unprofessional approach — not a lack of talent. Agents see hundreds of applications. The ones that get a response are the ones that feel deliberate, polished, and easy to act on.

"
If you can't put together a basic professional email, what chance is there that you'll read your schedule, show up on time, dress right, and be reliable on a ship? The email is a proxy for on-board reliability.

— The agent's logic

⚠️ Warning: Some agents auto-forward your submission immediately

Some agents forward your promo to every cruise line the moment they receive it — to "claim ownership" of you as their artist. That means a sloppy, rushed submission can get you burned across the entire market at once. Be deliberate about who you send to and make sure every submission represents you well.

❌ Avoid
  • • Blasting one email to 50 agents in CC
  • • "Dear Agent, available from tomorrow"
  • • Generic spray-and-pray emails
  • • Strangely formatted or forwarded messages
  • • Sending before your materials are ready
✓ Be Surgical, Not Spray-and-Pray
  • • Research and target agents intentionally
  • • One email per agent, written just for them
  • • Use their name if you have it
  • • Send only when your reel and materials are polished
  • • Be specific about your position and availability
✅ What a Good First Email Looks Like

Simple, honest, personal. Gets straight to the point.

Hi [Agent Name], My name is [Your Name]. I'm a [piano bar entertainer / guitar vocalist] looking for cruise ship contracts. Here's my reel: [public YouTube link] My song list is attached. I'm available from [Month Year]. [Optional — if you're new:] I don't yet have cruise ship experience, but I'd welcome your guidance on what you're looking for. [Your Name] · [Phone] · [Email]

💡 Honesty about experience level is completely fine — agents respect it. What they don't respect is vague, impersonal, or disorganised.

How to Choose a Trustworthy Agent

Before you sign anything — understand commission, red flags, and the rules that protect you.

How to Choose a Trustworthy Agent

✅ Trust Signals — a good agent:
  • ✓ Is 100% transparent about money — you always know your fee AND their commission
  • ✓ Is responsive and reachable — you can actually get hold of them
  • ✓ Cares about your goals, not just filling a slot
  • ✓ Puts everything in a clear written agreement
  • ✓ Welcomes your questions without being defensive
🚩 Red Flags — walk away if:
  • ✗ "We never take commission" — this is a lie
  • ✗ Vague terms or pressure to sign fast
  • ✗ Slow/no communication before you've even signed
  • ✗ "Exclusivity forever" with no clear terms
  • ✗ You can't find any trace of their placed musicians

💰 How agent commission actually works

Agents are typically paid by the cruise line, not by you directly — but you feel it in your salary. The line pays the agent a rate (for example €1,200/week), and the agent pays you less (for example €800/week), keeping the difference. A transparent agent will tell you both numbers upfront. The industry-standard commission for solo cruise musicians is around 15%. Anything much higher is worth questioning. Anything hidden is unacceptable.

Note: "We don't charge commission" is a lie — it just means the split is hidden. Demand clarity on both numbers.

⚠️ The First-Submitter Rule — critical to understand

Most cruise lines honour the first agent to submit a musician and ignore later submissions. This creates a critical rule for how you manage your applications:

  • • If two agents submit you to the same line, there will be a dispute — and it damages your reputation with both agents and the line
  • • Lines sometimes "forget" they've seen someone, which is why agents guard their submissions carefully
  • • Being a "fresh" talent presented by only one agent is an advantage — protect that
The rule to follow:

Always tell each agent exactly which other agencies or cruise lines you have already been submitted to. Never let two agents unknowingly submit you to the same line. This honesty protects you and builds trust with agents.

How to Follow Up

Following up is essential. Here's exactly when to do it, what to say, and what to add to your calendar so nothing falls through the cracks.

The Follow-Up Timeline

14d

14 Days After Submission

Send a brief, friendly follow-up. One or two sentences is enough — you're just confirming they received your materials.

30d

30 Days After Submission

One final follow-up. Keep it short, reiterate your interest, then move on. Don't chase after this point.

3mo

3–4 Months Later

If you haven't heard back, consider reapplying with updated or improved materials. A fresh submission is better than a third follow-up.

📅 Add These Reminders to Your Calendar

For every agency you apply to, create these three reminders in Google Calendar, Outlook, or your planner:

📌 Day 0: "Applied to [Agency Name]" — note the date and what you sent
📧 Day 14: "Follow up with [Agency Name]" — use Template 2
📧 Day 30: "Final follow-up with [Agency Name]" — use Template 3
🔄 Day 90: "Consider reapplying to [Agency Name]" — use Template 4

What to Track

Use a simple spreadsheet or notebook. You only need four columns:

Agency Name

Which agency did you apply to?

Date Submitted

When did you send the application?

Follow-up Dates

When did you follow up?

Response Status

Did they reply? What happened?

💡 Follow-Up Best Practices

Don't take it personally. Agencies receive hundreds of applications — silence is not a rejection. Keep your tone friendly and professional at all times. After two follow-ups with no response, move on and reapply in 3–4 months if you want. Never send more than two follow-ups to the same agency per application cycle.
